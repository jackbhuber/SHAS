\hypertarget{about-this-project}{%
\chapter{About this project}\label{about-this-project}}

This is reproducible research project for validation of the nascent Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS), developed by Dan Koch. It aims to function as an evolving technical report to conceptualize and document various validity studies -- and, by extension, validity evidence -- for this scale. We hope some of these validity studies may amount to meaningful publishable contributions to the nascent research literature in spiritual abuse and/or the literature in religion, counseling psychology, and/or psychological measurement.

Following is an initial set of questions to guide research and scale improvement:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Item refinement} - Do some items have too many response categories?
\item
  \textbf{Data reduction} - As written and executed, which items appear to be critical (best performing) and which appear to be redundant or noisy? What might a shorter, streamlined instrument look like?
\item
  \textbf{Person fit} - How well does the current scale fit the population of examinees? Are there ceiling or floor effects? To what extent might the existing items under-represent the full range of abuse?
\item
  \textbf{Scale invariance} - Do the scales mean the same thing to different groups of examinees? To what extent do these scales (abuse constructs) vary by age, gender, and/or sexuality?
\item
  \textbf{Developmental pathway} - Beyond the current conceptualization of scales composed of redundant items, what an incremental scale of abuse with diagnostic items look like?
\end{itemize}

This plot reveal considerable missing data in the items toward the end of the dataset: those intended to measure internal states.

The important question with missing data is whether the missingness is random or systematic.

The fact that most of the missing data are toward the \emph{end} is a pattern. After thoughtfully responding to the 52 preceding items about abusive external events, at least some examinees were tired of answering questions. The sensitive nature of the internal states items may have been especially triggering to some examinees. One way to investigate this possibility is to examine the association between the external states items and whether examinees responded to the internal states items.

The following code chunk conceives and executes a simple test of the triggering hypothesis. The items measuring external events are summed into a total score such that a higher value indicates more exposure to abusive external events. The items measuring internal states are coded so that 1=missing and 0=all others response.

\hypertarget{missing-data-methods}{%
\subsection{Missing data methods}\label{missing-data-methods}}

There are several different ways to handle missing data. A common method is to \emph{delete all cases missing any data}. This is called \emph{listwise deletion} of data. Its primary advantages are simplicity, expedience, and analysis based on complete information. The disadvantages of listwise, however, are (1) disposal of the information offered by those examinees who responded to some items but not others, and (2) diminishment of the dataset, and with it, a reduction in statistical power.

In this case, the original dataset includes data from 3,222 examinees. A subset based on complete data (listwise deletion of all examinees missing any data) would be only 746 examinees -- a reduction of 76\%. In the following code chunk I select several datasets from listwise deletion.

\hypertarget{confirmatory-factor-analysis-cfa}{%
\section{Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)}\label{confirmatory-factor-analysis-cfa}}

Factor analysis is commonly used to investigate whether item responses are unidimensional. In my use of CFA I used several statistics and fit indices:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Root Mean Square Error of Approximation (RMSEA)
\item
  Comparative Fit Index (CFI)
\item
  Tucker--Lewis Index (TLI), and
\item
  Standardized Root-Mean-Square Residual (SRMR)
\end{itemize}

Based on published criteria (Hu \& Bentler, 1999; Wang \& Wang, 2019), I used the following standards for good fit:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  CFI \textgreater{} 0.95
\item
  TLI \textgreater{} 0.95
\item
  RMSEA \textless{} 0.05
\item
  SRMR \textless{} 0.08
\end{itemize}
