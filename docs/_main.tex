\hypertarget{abstract}{%
\section{Abstract}\label{abstract}}

This is a reproducible research project for validation of the nascent Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS), developed by Dan Koch. It aims to function as an evolving technical report to conceptualize and document various validity studies -- and, by extension, validity evidence -- for this scale. We hope some of these validity considerations and empirical studies amount to meaningful publishable contributions to the nascent research literature in spiritual abuse and/or the literature in religion, counseling psychology, and/or psychological measurement.

The project is open source, for collaboration. It is built in R using the R Studio IDE \citep{R-base}. No Excel, no SPSS, no Word. It is built in \texttt{bookdown} (\citet{R-bookdown}) and hosted at Github as a public repository at \url{https://github.com/jackbhuber/SHAS}. There it is served over Github Pages as a website -- \url{https://jackhuber.org/SHAS/} -- for collaboration between Jack and Dan.

\textbf{Research Questions}

Validation is research into the meanings and uses of scores from instruments. It can be an ongoing research agenda. Following is an initial set of questions to guide research and scale improvement of the SHAS:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Item analysis and refinement} - Do some items have too many response categories?
\item
  \textbf{Data reduction} - As written and executed, which items appear to be critical (best performing) and which appear to be redundant or noisy? What might a shorter, streamlined instrument look like?
\item
  \textbf{Person fit} - How well does the current scale fit the population of examinees? Are there ceiling or floor effects? To what extent might the existing items under-represent the full range of abuse?
\item
  \textbf{Scale invariance} - Do the scales mean the same thing to different groups of examinees? To what extent do these scales (abuse constructs) vary by age, gender, and/or sexuality?
\item
  \textbf{Developmental pathway} - Beyond the current conceptualization of scales composed of redundant items, what might an incremental scale of abuse with diagnostic items look like?
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{introduction}{%
\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}}

Spiritual and religious abuse is a serious problem that is gaining the attention of the psychological research community. \citet{KochEdstrom2022} define spiritual abuse as ``a type of emotional and psychological abuse perpetrated by a religious leader or group and/or with a religious or spiritual component, usually involving coercion or control.'' Religious and spiritual abuse is distinguished from other forms of abuse by a religious component of one sort or another. A form of emotional and psychological abuse characterized by systemic patterns of coercion and control in religious contexts; spiritual abuse is distinguished from broad emotional abuse by its common inclusion of specifically religious elements, like the quoting of scripture or the giving of a ``divine rationale'' for mistreatment (Oakley et al, 2018). Categories: rejecting, isolating, terrorizing, ignoring, corrupting, verbally assaulting, and over-pressuring (Pearl, 1994). Three broad categories of religious abuse: abuse perpetrated by religious leaders, abuse perpetrated by a religious group or person representing a religious group, and abuse with a religious or spiritual component (Swindle, 2017). Other definitions focus on specific forms of religious or spiritual abuse, like using scripture or religious language to justify domestic abuse (Cares \& Cusick, 2012; Warford, 2014), any sexual or physically abusive act done by a clergy member (Doyle, 2006), and theologically sanctioned mistreatment of LGBTQ persons (Foster, Bowland, \& Vosler, 2015).

Research suggests spiritual and religious abuse has serious negative effects (Ward, 2011; Swindle, 2017; Oakley \& Kinmond, 2014; Stevens et al.~2019). Religious leaders may be on a par with parents in their potential for abusive harm. Further, trauma that takes place in a religious or spiritual setting may deprive victims of religion and spirituality as a coping strategy. There are also concerns related to secondary trauma for clinicians working with R/S abused clients (Gubi \& Jacobs, 2009).

In light of these concerns, a valid scale of spiritual harm and abuse is needed. Practitioners treating clients whom they suspect may have been victims of religious and spiritual abuse need an instrument as part of their intake process, enabling them to evaluate their client's score in light of established norms, and researchers need a reliable measurement of exposure to potential spiritual abuse and its effects for research purposes, looking for relationships between spiritual abuse and any number of other psychological, health, or other measures.

\citet{Keller2016} developed a scale to measure religious or spiritual abuse but has yet to publish psychometric evidence of its technical quality. Other scales measure various types of abuse, but not religious and spiritual abuse specifically (Kira, et al.~2008; Sanders \& Becker-Lausen, 1995; Carlson, et al.~2011; Briere \& Runtz, 1989). \citet{KochEdstrom2022} laid the foundation for a scale measuring exposure to potentially spiritually abusive experiences. They asked which types of religious and spiritual abuse most succinctly capture the phenomenon of being broadly exposed to religious and spiritual abuse. They defined the construct of spiritual harm and abuse and developed an initial item pool. They conducted EFA which revealed a handful of factors which they used to reduce the number of items and to define several subscales.

This paper builds on \citet{KochEdstrom2022} and gathers additional validity evidence for the SHAS by using item response theory.

\begin{quote}
Although more commonly used with achievement tests, IRT models can also be used for both item calibration and ``ability'' estimation with rating scale instruments such as questionnaires measuring psychological or behavioral constructs. There are several advantages to using IRT models with rating scale instruments compared to other approaches such as sum scoring. Most importantly, such instruments yield data that are ordinal, not interval, making use of sum scores questionable (Smith, Conrad, Chang, \& Piazza, 2002). In addition, IRT models provide ``sample-free measurement estimates, making it possible to estimate a person's level of the latent construct free of the distribution of the individual items and to estimate an item's difficulty level free from the distribtion of people used in the sample'' (DiStefano and Morgan, 2011, p.356). In addition, as Reeve and Masse (2004) point out, these models allow more in-depth analysis of items, examination of precision across the score continuum (as opposed to a single overall reliability coefficient) and better handling of complex measurement problems such as linking scores across alternative forms'' \citep[p.2]{Dogan2018}.
\end{quote}

The goal of this study was to refine the SHAS and provide an outcome measure that reflects a patient's exposure to and damage from spiritual harm and abuse. We aimed to establish an item bank on spiritual harm and abuse that is sufficiently uni-dimensional to yield a single score, is free from differential item functioning based on race, gender, and maximizes parsimony and precision across the continuum of the latent construct.

\hypertarget{methods}{%
\section{Methods}\label{methods}}

\hypertarget{item-pool-development}{%
\subsection{Item Pool Development}\label{item-pool-development}}

\citet{KochEdstrom2022} developed a Spiritual Harm and Abuse item pool. They validated the spiritual harm and abuse construct using a literature review, expert reviews, and qualitative concept elicitation interviews with people. The item pool for this study (66 items) was generated by mapping items from publicly available measures of similar constructs and writing new items to achieve construct coverage. Cognitive interviews with X individuals included think aloud responses, prompts for comprehension, retrieval, judgement or other difficulties responding to items, and relevance. The a priori standard for item inclusion was \ldots{} three consecutive interviews establishing comprehension, relevance, and no difficulties.

\hypertarget{participants-and-procedures}{%
\subsection{Participants and Procedures}\label{participants-and-procedures}}

We conducted an online survey of adults who had identified as Christian in the past. Inclusion criteria were: at least 18 years of age and past self-identification as a Christian. Sampling was by convenience. Dan sent the link out to a lot of people, including the You Have Permission Facebook community, and encouraged others to distribute the link widely. The sampling priorities for this initial quantitative validation were (a) variability across the full range of the construct; and (b) representation of diverse clinical samples from target populations for the measure's use. Participants were consented before beginning the survey. The study was approved by the IRB at Northwest University. The data were collected from January 28, 2021 to February 27, 2021.
\#\# Measures

\textbf{Spiritual Harm and Abuse items}

\citet{KochEdstrom2022} tested 66 candidate items representing the two domains of the spiritual harm and abuse construct: external events (52 items) and internal states (14 items). The measure asks respondents to rate the frequency with which they experienced each event or state. Items were scored on a 5-point Likert scale with response options of ``Never,'' ``Once or twice,'' ``Sometimes,'' ``Often,'' and ``All the time.''

\hypertarget{data-analysis}{%
\subsection{Data Analysis}\label{data-analysis}}

Our analysis strategy was to distill the item pool to reflect the latent construct of spiritual and religious abuse. Although our theoretical model was composed of two inter-related domains of external abuse events and internal states, we hypothesized that spiritual abuse stemmed from generalized harm across these domains, making the common variance across domains our primary interest rather than comprehensive coverage of each domain that would result in separate scales or subscales. We assumed an effect-indicator model from the beginning of the analysis, and therefore, the analysis strategy also targeted removal of smaller, extraneous factors in an effort to identify a sufficiently unidimensional set of items that could form a single scale. Different theoretical assumptions such as creating an inventory of abuse and damage could be consistent with a causal-indicator model where items are selected to maximize the unique content represented by each subdomain. While there are merits to each approach, the latter would have contributed to proliferation of similar constructs in the field, overemphasized each subdomain's independence, increased respondent burden, and would have had relatively limited utility in clinical settings due to the need to reliably assess and interpret multiple domains and scores.

We began by examining classical item-level statistics, polychoric correlations, and hierarchical cluster analysis to identify poorly functioning items. Next, we conducted iterative exploratory bifactor analyses using the psych package in R. We started by selecting two subfactors corresponding to the two hypothesized domains of the construct but considered solutions with three subfactors as well. We evaluated dimensionality based on general factor saturation and computed Omega-Hierarchical (Omega-H), where values \textgreater{} 0.70 suggest sufficient unidimensionality for IRT modeling {[}36{]}. In addition, we calculated explained common variance (ECV), the relative strength of a general factor compared to group factors, targeting ECV values \textgreater{} 0.60 as a tentative benchmark {[}36{]}.

To illustrate the structure of our final set of items prior to IRT analyses, we ran a confirmatory bifactor model corresponding to the final structure determined by exploratory factor analyses. For this final model, we report Omega-H, ECV, Comparative Fit Index (CFI), the Tucker Lewis Index (TLI), and the root-mean-square error of approximation (RMSEA). Next, we estimated IRT parameters using the graded response model (GRM) as well as the corresponding IRT-based bifactor model with three subfactors. We did this with the understanding that unidimensional parameters might still be distorted by subfactors evident in a bifactor model. Therefore, we applied a procedure to convert conditional general factor slopes into marginal slopes, using formulas 10 and 11 provided in a didactic article by Toland et al.~{[}43{]}, citing detailed work on these procedures {[}44{]}. Once in marginal form, the slopes and location parameters from the bifactor model can be fairly compared to unidimensional models, and may be scored ``as if'' they were from unidimensional models.

Differential item functioning (DIF) analysis compared race (Black/African-American vs.~White), age (65 and above vs below 65), etc. We applied logistic ordinal regression with IRT scoring, commonly used in the development of PROMIS instruments {[}45{]}. We used the Chi-squared likelihood-ratio statistic as the initial DIF detection criteria (alpha \textless{} 0.01), and a cut-off of McFadden pseudo R2Δ ≥ 0.02 in model comparisons to determine substantial DIF, a reasonable threshold used in the development of self-reported health outcomes {[}46{]}.

All analyses were conducted in \textbf{R}, using the \textbf{psych} package {[}47{]} and \textbf{lavaan} {[}48{]} for factor analyses, \textbf{mirt} {[}49{]}for IRT analyses and \textbf{lordif} {[}45{]} for DIF analyses.

\hypertarget{initial-item-analysis}{%
\subsubsection{Initial Item Analysis}\label{initial-item-analysis}}

\begin{quote}
XXX pairs of items in the polychoric correlation matrix were highly correlated (r \textgreater{} 0.70). After removing X items, these pairs disappeared (several items appeared in multiple pairs). We note that these pairs of items were expected because we tested several items with alternate wording. Next, we examined how individual items (or small groups of items) might separate from larger sets of items with a hierarchical item cluster plot {[}34{]}. This plot showed final clustering of a pair of items with narrow content (social support) and a single item that had poor wording and the lowest cluster loading (i.e., corrected item-total correlation). We deleted these three items, and an additional item with redundant wording, leaving XX in the item pool at this stage.
\end{quote}

\hypertarget{exploratory-bifactor-analysis}{%
\subsection{Exploratory Bifactor Analysis}\label{exploratory-bifactor-analysis}}

\begin{quote}
We removed a total of nine items in this step, based on empirical and conceptual considerations. The bifactor models with four subfactors identified three items with low general factor loadings (\textless{} 0.50) or high specific factor loadings (\textgreater{} 0.50). These items focused on individual health behavior maintenance. In addition, we noticed that a single specific factor was driven by three items from the ``health information use'' domain, also showing high specific factor loadings and low general factor loadings, suggesting a potential for multidimensionality. They focused on independent health information use (e.g., ``It is easy to find information on my own that helps me manage my symptoms''). All other items in this domain showed lower loadings on the ``health information use'' factor (\textless{} 0.35). We opted to remove these three items, and model three subfactors representing the remaining subdomains. This decision to shift from 4 to 3 subfactors was based not only on these empirical considerations, but on the overarching conceptual assumption that the construct of engagement is essentially collaborative, and that the multidimensionality of items addressing independent skills was inconsistent with this assumption.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
In the subsequent 3-factor bifactor model, we identified three more items with general factor loadings of 0.50 or less. These items addressed resilient self-efficacy for independent self-management behaviors (e.g., ``I attend all of my appointments, even when life gets busy or stressful'') and were removed based on similar empirical considerations and conceptual assumptions. The resulting 30 item set showed a high proportion of general factor variance relative to specific factor variance (item-level proportions ranging from 0.58 to 0.83), with overall indices of Omega-H as 0.79 and ECV 0.69. These values suggest sufficient levels of unidimensionality and that the items mostly reflect a single construct {[}36{]}.
\end{quote}
