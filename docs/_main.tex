\hypertarget{abstract}{%
\section{Abstract}\label{abstract}}

Spiritual harm and abuse has been a serious problem that has begun to gain the attention of the clinical and psychological research community. Some research has documented lasting emotional and psychological damage to victims of spiritual abuse. A valid scale of spiritual and religious abuse is needed, and the nascent Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS) has potential to meet this need. \citet{KochEdstrom2022} developed an item pool and conducted an initial validation study of the SHAS which established the multidimensional structure of the item pool. This study builds on \citet{KochEdstrom2022} to further validate the SHAS by using item response theory (IRT). The goal of this study was to enhance the SHAS with several of these features of IRT. Provided that the items were sufficiently unidimensional, we aimed to express the SHAS as a single interval-level outcome measure of a patient's lifetime exposure to, and damage from, spiritual harm and abuse. Because in IRT the scale describes items as well as persons, we aimed to further develop the potential diagnostic value of the SHAS items by illustrating how they individually assign real-world meaning to locations on the scale as well how they cluster with other items to measure some parts of the scale with more precision than others. Such a scale also lays the foundation to further develop the SHAS by identifying anchor items that can calibrate with pilot items on future forms. Finally, we aimed to establish a SHAS score that is free of differential item functioning based on race, gender, and other demographic variables, and maximizes parsimony and precision across the continuum of the latent construct.

\hypertarget{introduction}{%
\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}}

Spiritual and religious abuse has been a serious problem which has begun to gain the attention of the psychological research community. \citet{KochEdstrom2022} define spiritual abuse as ``a type of emotional and psychological abuse perpetrated by a religious leader or group and/or with a religious or spiritual component, usually involving coercion or control.'' Religious and spiritual abuse is distinguished from other forms of abuse by a religious component of one sort or another. A form of emotional and psychological abuse characterized by systemic patterns of coercion and control in religious contexts; spiritual abuse is distinguished from broad emotional abuse by its common inclusion of specifically religious elements, like the quoting of scripture or the giving of a ``divine rationale'' for mistreatment (Oakley et al, 2018). Categories: rejecting, isolating, terrorizing, ignoring, corrupting, verbally assaulting, and over-pressuring (Pearl, 1994). Three broad categories of religious abuse: abuse perpetrated by religious leaders, abuse perpetrated by a religious group or person representing a religious group, and abuse with a religious or spiritual component (Swindle, 2017). Other definitions focus on specific forms of religious or spiritual abuse, like using scripture or religious language to justify domestic abuse (Cares \& Cusick, 2012; Warford, 2014), any sexual or physically abusive act done by a clergy member (Doyle, 2006), and theologically sanctioned mistreatment of LGBTQ persons (Foster, Bowland, \& Vosler, 2015).

Research suggests spiritual and religious abuse has serious negative effects (Ward, 2011; Swindle, 2017; Oakley \& Kinmond, 2014; Stevens et al.~2019). Religious leaders may be on a par with parents in their potential for abusive harm. Further, trauma that takes place in a religious or spiritual setting may deprive victims of religion and spirituality as a coping strategy. There are also concerns related to secondary trauma for clinicians working with R/S abused clients (Gubi \& Jacobs, 2009).

In light of these concerns, a valid scale of spiritual harm and abuse is needed. Practitioners treating clients whom they suspect have been victims of religious and spiritual abuse need an instrument for clinical use. Clinicians could use such a tool as part of the intake process, to evaluate their client's score in light of established norms. Researchers need a reliable measurement of exposure to potential spiritual abuse and its effects for research purposes, looking for relationships between spiritual abuse and any number of other psychological, health, or other measures.

Drawing from a recent unpublished scale of religious and abuse \citep{Keller2016} and other scales of abuse (Kira, et al.~2008; Sanders \& Becker-Lausen, 1995; Carlson, et al.~2011; Briere \& Runtz, 1989), \citet{KochEdstrom2022} developed the Spiritual Harm \& Abuse Scale (SHAS). Asking which types of religious and spiritual abuse most succinctly capture the phenomenon of being broadly exposed to religious and spiritual abuse, they defined the construct of spiritual harm and abuse and developed an initial item pool. Their study of the factor structure revealed a handful of factors which they used to reduce the number of items and to define several subscales.

In this paper, we build on \citet{KochEdstrom2022} to further validate the SHAS by using item response theory (IRT). \citet{Dogan2018} describes several applications of IRT for measurement scales such as SHAS based on Likert item rating scales:

\begin{quote}
There are several advantages to using IRT models with rating scale instruments compared to other approaches such as sum scoring. Most importantly, such instruments yield data that are ordinal, not interval, making use of sum scores questionable (Smith, Conrad, Chang, \& Piazza, 2002). In addition, IRT models provide ``sample-free measurement estimates, making it possible to estimate a person's level of the latent construct free of the distribution of the individual items and to estimate an item's difficulty level free from the distribtion of people used in the sample'' (DiStefano and Morgan, 2011, p.356). In addition, as Reeve and Masse (2004) point out, these models allow more in-depth analysis of items, examination of precision across the score continuum (as opposed to a single overall reliability coefficient) and better handling of complex measurement problems such as linking scores across alternative forms'' (p.2).
\end{quote}

The goal of this study was to enhance the SHAS with several of these features of IRT. Provided that the items were sufficiently unidimensional, we aimed to express the SHAS as a single interval-level outcome measure of a patient's lifetime exposure to, and damage from, spiritual harm and abuse. Because in IRT the scale describes items as well as persons, we aimed to further develop the potential diagnostic value of the SHAS items by illustrating how they individually assign real-world meaning to locations on the scale as well how they cluster with other items to measure some parts of the scale with more precision than others. Such a scale also lays the foundation to further develop the SHAS by identifying anchor items that can calibrate with pilot items on future forms. Finally, we aimed to establish a SHAS score that is free of differential item functioning based on race, gender, and other demographic variables, and maximizes parsimony and precision across the continuum of the latent construct.

\hypertarget{methods}{%
\section{Methods}\label{methods}}

\hypertarget{item-pool-development}{%
\subsection{Item Pool Development}\label{item-pool-development}}

\citet{KochEdstrom2022} developed an initial pool of items to measure spiritual harm and abuse. The authors validated the construct of spiritual harm and abuse through literature review (Kvarfordt, 2010; Nobakht \& Dale, 2018; Oakley \& Kinmond, 2014; Rodríguez-Carballeira, et al.~2015; Swindle, 2017; Ward, 2011, Winell, n.d.) and interviews with survivors of religious and spiritual abuse. These interviews revealed an array of specific examples of potential abuse not described in the published literature such as abuse inflicted by narcissistic persons, financial coercion, developmentally inappropriate children's teachings, pressure to stay in physically abusive marriages, neglect of needed medical care, sexual discrimination, and shunning/shaming, among others. The authors generated items by drawing from publicly available measures of similar constructs and by writing new items to maximize construct coverage. Each item they adapted to measure either a potentially spiritually abusive event (inflicting emotional abuse by a religious leader or group and/or with a religious or spiritual component, usually involving coercion or control) or a theorized effect of spiritual abuse, based on the reports of self-described survivors of spiritual abuse in the qualitative literature. In developing items, the authors sought to balance construct coverage with alternate wording and efficiency for participants. They wanted to cover as much of the domain as possible in a survey that could be completed in 15 minutes.

\hypertarget{participants-and-procedures}{%
\subsection{Participants and Procedures}\label{participants-and-procedures}}

Participants in the initial SHAS validation study were adults who responded to an online survey. To qualify for the survey, participants had to be at least 18 years of age and they had to have identified themselves as Christian in the past \citep{KochEdstrom2022}. Koch promoted the survey through various podcast feeds (in audio form during a podcast episode), email lists, Facebook groups, and other online groups associated with progressive Christian podcasts based in the United States. Listeners/readers were encouraged to invite others to take part in the survey, so long as they satisfied the above criteria. Koch also posted the link on his own social media accounts with a similar encouragement to invite others who satisfied the study criteria. The sampling priority was to reach the largest, most inclusive, community sample. Participants were consented before beginning the survey. The study was approved by the IRB at Northwest University. Data collection began on January 28, 2021 and concluded on February 27, 2021.

\hypertarget{measures}{%
\subsection{Measures}\label{measures}}

\textbf{Spiritual Harm and Abuse items}

The final survey instrument included a total of 66 prompts separated into two distinct categories \citep{KochEdstrom2022}. The first section, External Events, included 52 prompts about potentially abusive experiences. Prompts in this section measured prevalence by asking ``How often have you experienced the above in a church or Christian group setting?'' (5-point Likert scale: never, once or twice, sometimes, often, all the time). The large number of prompts in this first section were presented in a randomized order so that all prompts would receive responses by approximately the same number of participants even if many participants did not complete all of the prompts in this section. The second section, Internal States, included 14 prompts about personal feelings resulting from the abusive experiences described in the first section. Prompts in this section measured prevalence by asking ``At any point, how often have you experienced the above as a result of negative religious experiences?'' (5-point Likert scale: never, once or twice, sometimes, often, all the time).

\hypertarget{data-analysis}{%
\subsection{Data Analysis}\label{data-analysis}}

In their initial validation of the SHAS, \citet{KochEdstrom2022} examined the dimensionality of the items. The authors used exploratory factor analysis (EFA) to determine the factor structure of the items. In these analyses, the items loaded onto X inter-related factors which helped the authors distill items and identify several subscales for potential future use by clinicians and researchers.

For this study, we shifted our focused to the commonality of the items for the purpose of item response theory. Item response theory models make two assumptions: that a single latent trait explains responses to items (unidimensionality) and that, after controlling for this latent trait, items are weakly correlated (local independence) \citep{EmbretsonReise2000, ReiseWaller2009, ReiseBonifayHaviland2013}. The SHAS items were multidimensional by design. The construct of spiritual harm and abuse is not conceptually narrow, and to cover the breadth of this construct Koch wrote two distinct item sets. However, a unidimensional IRT model can still be fit to multidimensional data \citep{ReiseMooreHaviland2013}. In this study we hypothesized a general factor of spiritual abuse as the primary source of the common variance across the 66 SHAS items. Therefore, we sought evidence of unidimensionality and local dependence in order to calibrate the items and participants to a single IRT scale.

We began by examining classical item-level statistics, polychoric correlations, and hierarchical cluster analysis to identify poorly functioning items. Next, we conducted iterative exploratory bifactor analyses using the psych package in R. We started by selecting two subfactors corresponding to the two hypothesized domains of the construct. We evaluated dimensionality based on general factor saturation and computed Omega-Hierarchical (Omega-H), where values \textgreater{} 0.70 suggest sufficient unidimensionality for IRT modeling \citep{ReiseBonifayHaviland2013}. We also calculated explained common variance (ECV), the relative strength of a general factor compared to group factors, targeting ECV values \textgreater{} 0.60 as a tentative benchmark \citep{ReiseBonifayHaviland2013}.

To illustrate the structure of our final set of items prior to IRT analyses, we ran a confirmatory bifactor model corresponding to the final structure determined by exploratory factor analyses. For this final model, we report Omega-H, ECV, Comparative Fit Index (CFI), the Tucker Lewis Index (TLI), and the root-mean-square error of approximation (RMSEA). Next, we estimated IRT parameters using the graded response model (GRM) as well as the corresponding IRT-based bifactor model with X subfactors. We did this with the understanding that unidimensional parameters might still be distorted by subfactors evident in a bifactor model. Therefore, we applied a procedure to convert conditional general factor slopes into marginal slopes, using formulas 10 and 11 provided in a didactic article by Toland et al.~{[}43{]}, citing detailed work on these procedures {[}44{]}. Once in marginal form, the slopes and location parameters from the bifactor model can be fairly compared to unidimensional models, and may be scored ``as if'' they were from unidimensional models.

Differential item functioning (DIF) analysis compared race (Black/African-American vs.~White), age (65 and above vs below 65), etc. We applied logistic ordinal regression with IRT scoring, commonly used in the development of PROMIS instruments {[}45{]}. We used the Chi-squared likelihood-ratio statistic as the initial DIF detection criteria (alpha \textless{} 0.01), and a cut-off of McFadden pseudo R2Δ ≥ 0.02 in model comparisons to determine substantial DIF, a reasonable threshold used in the development of self-reported health outcomes {[}46{]}.

All analyses were conducted in \textbf{R} \citep{R-base}, using the \textbf{psych} package \citep{R-psych} and \textbf{lavaan} \citep{R-lavaan} for factor analyses, \textbf{mirt} \citep{R-mirt} for IRT analyses and \textbf{lordif} \citep{R-lordif} for DIF analyses.

\section{Results}\label{results}}

\hypertarget{participant-characteristics}{%
\subsection{Participant Characteristics}\label{participant-characteristics}}

In total, 3,222 individuals responded to the survey. Participants who did not opt out and completed at least 50\% of the spiritual abuse item pool were considered complete responders and included in the study (N=3,219). Non-responders included those who responded to less than half of the items (N=3).

Almost all participants (N = 3,064) answered demographics items, and the results are presented Table 1 below. Compared to the general U.S. population, the sample was largely white (86\%) and Protestant (57\%). The average respondent saw themselves as slightly theologically liberal (M = 4.5, range 1-7). About 4 in 5 identified as straight (82\%), and most were raised in a Christian home (87\%).

\citet{KochEdstrom2022} conducted Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin (KMO) tests on the 61 items to determine the degree of shared variance between the items. The KMO was .987, well above the .5 threshold required to proceed.

\citet{KochEdstrom2022} conducted alpha factoring with Varimax rotation on the 61 items. Alpha Factoring is most appropriate when variables are not assumed to be definitive but rather are a group of variables out of a larger set of possible variables (Rovai et al., 2014). Considering that the 61 remaining prompts were taken from scant qualitative research, anecdotes, and personal experience, many other variables might have also been included to measure and describe the concept of spiritual abuse. Best practices, in this case, include running both Varimax and Promax rotations, rather than making a priori assumptions about the degree of correlation between the underlying factors of the concept being studied (Tinsley \& Tinsley, 1987). They focused on independent health information use (e.g., ``It is easy to find information on my own that helps me manage my symptoms''). All other items in this domain showed lower loadings on the ``health information use'' factor (\textless{} 0.35). We opted to remove these three items, and model three subfactors representing the remaining subdomains. This decision to shift from 4 to 3 subfactors was based not only on these empirical considerations, but on the overarching conceptual assumption that the construct of engagement is essentially collaborative, and that the multidimensionality of items addressing independent skills was inconsistent with this assumption.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}}

\begin{quote}
The results supported the hypothesis that fewer than 30 items across fewer than 10 factors would be found to comprise a spiritual abuse scale with adequate reliability. In the end, 27 items over 6 factors were found to comprise the new Spiritual Harm \& Abuse Scale:
- Maintaining the System
- Internal Distress
- Embracing Violence
- Controlling Leadership
- Harmful God-image
- Gender Discrimination
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
There is likely to be convergent validity with the sole existing similar scale. For the Spiritual Abuse Questionnaire (SAQ), Keller (2016) found the following 4 factors: Abuse of Power, Conditionality, Spiritual Injury, and Suppression of Expression. Some overlap can be seen between the two sets of factors. For example, Spiritual Injury is indicated in both Internal Distress and Harmful God-image and Abuse of Power has face validity with both Controlling Leadership and Maintaining the System. Future research should evaluate correlations between these two scales and their subscales.
The highest factor correlation is between Controlling Leadership and Embracing Violence (.764), which may indicate that controlling leaders are more likely to use fear and terror to motivate religious decisions and loyalty. They may also be more likely to support or tolerate violence, either real-life or biblical accounts of violence, to maintain their authority and power. More research should be done to support this conclusion, however. The next highest correlation is between Controlling Leadership and Internal Distress (.756). It may be that internal distress is caused by church communities with controlling leadership. However, the causal relationship may go the other way as well: Those who experience internal distress may rate their current or former church leadership as more authoritarian than those who do not experience such distress. Thirdly, some other cause may be responsible for elevated scores on both scales for many individuals.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Between the 6 factors and the additional ``critical items'' on the screener, all major abuse-related themes from the qualitative work of both Swindle (2017) and Ward (2011) appear to have at least one correlate on the SHAS, as shown in Table 7.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
The resulting scale can be used in clinical settings to quickly check a client's exposure to spiritual abuse and possible resulting consequences. It can be administered in roughly the same amount of time as short measures like the Beck Anxiety Inventory.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
The results of this research will comprise a scale and clinical screener for use by clinicians, potentially providing myriad benefits. Practitioners with clients whom they suspect may have been the victims of R/S abuse could administer the scale as part of their intake process, quickly able to check their client's score against established, valid, and reliable norms. The first section will comprise the 27 items, split into two sections. Subsection 1, focused on external events, will consist of 16 items under a heading that reads ``How often have you experienced the following in a church or Christian group setting?'' with possible answers scored 1-5 on a Likert scale: never, once or twice, sometimes, often, all the time. Subsection 2, focused on internal states, will consist of 11 items under a heading that reads ``In recent years, how often have you experienced the following as a result of negative religious experiences?'' with possible answers scored 1-5 on a Likert scale: never, once or twice, sometimes, often, all the time.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Section 2 will contain a heading ``Additionally, please check all that apply to your experience in Christian churches or groups throughout your life.'' The 11 critical items will be listed with a check box next to each. A scoring key will be included for the first section. Total scores and subscale scores for each of the six factors will be explained, with basic levels of severity indicated for a general population, based on mean and standard deviation information from this research. Clinicians will therefore be able to quickly assess how much potential spiritual abuse their client has been exposed to, as well as one or more focus areas based on subscale scores. The final screener is included as Appendix B.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Limitations and Future Research. One limitation of the SAS is that it was developed on a largely white and Protestant/former Protestant population. It is possible that major types or subthemes of spiritual abuse not captured here are more prominent in Catholic or non-white Christian settings, especially given that R/S can be such central forces in many of those groups. This would be a fruitful area of future study. Research looking at spiritual abuse among the LGBTQ+ community might also yield insights specific to that population. Future research could also distinguish normed scores of this scale for different populations and denominations.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Much additional analysis could also be done on the prevalence data contained in the survey responses. Comparisons could be made to the other existing Western quantitative spiritual abuse survey (Oakley \& Kinmond, 2014). Future research can look for correlations between exposure to R/S abuse and other religion-related measures, for example, the Religious Involvement Scale (Piedmont, et al.~2009), the Penn Inventory of Scrupulosity (Abrahamowitz et al, 2002), the Death Anxiety Scale (Templer, 1970), the Hell Anxiety Scale (Cranney, et al.~2017), and others. Comparisons can also be made with other trauma-related scales, like the Child Abuse and Trauma Scale, the Trauma History Screen, and the Cumulative Trauma Scale. Validity can also be established for both this new scale and the Spiritual Abuse Questionnaire (Keller, 2016) by correlating the two.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
The final screener has the potential to both improve therapy for innumerable clients who have experienced negative religious experiences and to contribute a knowledge base for any future clinical training around working with spiritually abused clients. The scale may also contribute to future research that could be critical in understanding not only the mechanisms and experience of spiritual abuse but also perhaps the enduring power of religion, both to heal and to harm.
\end{quote}
