\hypertarget{about-this-project}{%
\chapter{About this Project}\label{about-this-project}}

This is a reproducible research project for validation of the nascent Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS), developed by Dan Koch. It aims to function as an evolving technical report to conceptualize and document various validity studies -- and, by extension, validity evidence -- for this scale. We hope some of these validity studies amount to meaningful publishable contributions to the nascent research literature in spiritual abuse and/or the literature in religion, counseling psychology, and/or psychological measurement.

\hypertarget{research-questions}{%
\section{Research Questions}\label{research-questions}}

Validation can be an ongoing agenda for research into the meanings and uses of scores from instruments. Following is an initial set of questions to guide research and scale improvement of the SHAS:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Item analysis and refinement} - Do some items have too many response categories?
\item
  \textbf{Data reduction} - As written and executed, which items appear to be critical (best performing) and which appear to be redundant or noisy? What might a shorter, streamlined instrument look like?
\item
  \textbf{Person fit} - How well does the current scale fit the population of examinees? Are there ceiling or floor effects? To what extent might the existing items under-represent the full range of abuse?
\item
  \textbf{Scale invariance} - Do the scales mean the same thing to different groups of examinees? To what extent do these scales (abuse constructs) vary by age, gender, and/or sexuality?
\item
  \textbf{Developmental pathway} - Beyond the current conceptualization of scales composed of redundant items, what an incremental scale of abuse with diagnostic items look like?
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{data-collection}{%
\chapter{Data Collection}\label{data-collection}}

The data were collected online from January 28, 2021 to February 27, 2021.

A final total of 3,222 people responded to the survey at some level. Depending on how much data are missing, this is an impressively large sample size which will provide lots of power for most simple statistics and should for the most part satisfy the large-sample requirements of the advanced statistical procedures associated with measurement work (i.e., factor analysis, IRT).

However, the data come from a convenience sample which raises some challenge of sampling bias. Dan sent the link out to {[}lots of people{]}, and (if memory serves) encouraged them to send it to people \emph{they} knew. This would seem to bias responses toward people demographically similar to Dan: white, west coast American, middle class, highly educated, primarily Protestant and Catholic Christian, etc. This will be more apparent upon closer inspection of the demographics of the sample.

One source of examinees was people who listen to progressive Christians podcasts such as \emph{You Have Permission} which Dan produced at the time of data collection. This is a known source of bias toward people who have some interest in Christianity along with at least some level of rejection of conservative/orthodox Christianity and/or political conservatism. However, this would be an appropriate group from which to invite participation if the target population is people who have experienced spiritual abuse.

\hypertarget{missing-data}{%
\section{Missing data}\label{missing-data}}

Missing data can be an important problem. The problem has two parts. The first is how many data are missing. Obviously the more data are missing, the larger the barrier this problem poses to analysis. The second is the mechanism of missing data. Are the missing data randomly, or is there a pattern to the missing data - like a problematic item, or a distinct group of examinees who chose not to respond? Figure \ref{fig:vim-plots} presents two plots from the VIM package for missing data.

The first plot in Figure \ref{fig:vim-plots} is a frequency distribution of the number of missing data entries for each item. This plot reveals very few missing data for the first set of External Events items, but nearly 200 missing entries for the second set of Internal States items and demographics. The raises two questions. The first is: Who were those examinees? We know that they responded to the first 52 items, then apparently quit the survey. Why? Was it just fatigue, or is there more to know about them? The second question is what to do with the information they actually did provide.

The second plot in Figure \ref{fig:vim-plots} illustrates the different combinations of missing entries. The blue squares represent cases with complete responses, and the red indicates missing data. The rows represent combinations of complete and missing responses. The bottom row of entirely blue squares represents complete data -- examinees who responded to all of the items. The barplot on the right axis indicates that these were the most frequent combinations. The second most frequent combination was examinees who completed all of the External Events items and then quit the survey. Beyond that, there is no apparent order or pattern to the missing data.

\textbf{Mechanism of missing data}. \citet{McKnightEtAl2007} outline a variety of approaches for investigating the mechanisms of missing data. An appropriate first step is to test whether the data are Missing Completely At Random (MCAR) with the use of Little's test \citet{Little1988}.

\textbf{Were examinees triggered?} As mentioned above, a possible explanation of the missing data in the Internal States items is that examinees were triggered. As after responding to the preceding 52 items calling to mind imagery of abusive external events, these examinees were \textbf{done} with the survey. A possible test of this hypothesis is an examination of the relationship between a total score of responses to the External Events items and a score of total missingness on the Internal States items. The empirical work to test this hypothesis is laid out in the following code chunk. The items measuring external events are summed into a total score such that a higher value indicates more exposure to abusive external events. The items measuring internal states are coded so that 1=missing and 0=all others response, and these items are summed into a score of total missingness.

\textbf{Missing data methods}. There are several different ways to handle missing data. A common method is to \emph{delete all cases missing any data}. This is called \textbf{listwise} deletion of data. Its primary advantages are simplicity, expedience, and analysis based on complete information. The disadvantages of listwise are (1) disposal of the information offered by those examinees who responded to some items but not others, and (2) diminishment of the dataset, and with it, a reduction in statistical power. The ``all'' data file of all Likert items and demographics items derived earlier reports data from 3,222 examinees. The following code chunk creates a subset of complete data. This smaller dataset has 1,841 examinees.

\hypertarget{confirmatory-factor-analysis-cfa}{%
\section{Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)}\label{confirmatory-factor-analysis-cfa}}

Factor analysis is commonly used to investigate whether item responses are unidimensional. In my use of CFA I used several statistics and fit indices:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Root Mean Square Error of Approximation (RMSEA)
\item
  Comparative Fit Index (CFI)
\item
  Tucker--Lewis Index (TLI), and
\item
  Standardized Root-Mean-Square Residual (SRMR)
\end{itemize}

Based on published criteria (Hu \& Bentler, 1999; Wang \& Wang, 2019), I used the following standards for good fit:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  CFI \textgreater{} 0.95
\item
  TLI \textgreater{} 0.95
\item
  RMSEA \textless{} 0.05
\item
  SRMR \textless{} 0.08
\end{itemize}
