\hypertarget{about-this-project}{%
\chapter{About this Project}\label{about-this-project}}

This is a reproducible research project for validation of the nascent Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS), developed by Dan Koch. It aims to function as an evolving technical report to conceptualize and document various validity studies -- and, by extension, validity evidence -- for this scale. We hope some of these validity considerations and empirical studies amount to meaningful publishable contributions to the nascent research literature in spiritual abuse and/or the literature in religion, counseling psychology, and/or psychological measurement.

The project is built in R using the R Studio IDE. It is built in bookdown and hosted at \url{https://github.com/jackbhuber/SHAS} as a public repository. It is served out over Github Pages as a website for collaboration between Jack and Dan at \url{https://jackhuber.org/SHAS/}.

\hypertarget{research-questions}{%
\section{Research Questions}\label{research-questions}}

Validation can be an ongoing agenda for research into the meanings and uses of scores from instruments. Following is an initial set of questions to guide research and scale improvement of the SHAS:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Item analysis and refinement} - Do some items have too many response categories?
\item
  \textbf{Data reduction} - As written and executed, which items appear to be critical (best performing) and which appear to be redundant or noisy? What might a shorter, streamlined instrument look like?
\item
  \textbf{Person fit} - How well does the current scale fit the population of examinees? Are there ceiling or floor effects? To what extent might the existing items under-represent the full range of abuse?
\item
  \textbf{Scale invariance} - Do the scales mean the same thing to different groups of examinees? To what extent do these scales (abuse constructs) vary by age, gender, and/or sexuality?
\item
  \textbf{Developmental pathway} - Beyond the current conceptualization of scales composed of redundant items, what might an incremental scale of abuse with diagnostic items look like?
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{content-validity}{%
\chapter{Content Validity}\label{content-validity}}

In this chapter, we consider the content validity of the Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS). We begin by defining content validity and discussing its importance. Then we document the work we did to map the domain of spiritual abuse for the purpose of developing items for piloting and operational use. We conclude by considering what scope exists for more work in this area that would improve future development of SHAS instrumentation.

\hypertarget{about-content-validity}{%
\section{About Content Validity}\label{about-content-validity}}

The focus of content validity is ``the relationship between the content of a test and the construct it is intended to measure'' \citep{Standards}. Do they match? Does the instrument contain content that is not part of the construct? Are there parts of the content unmeasured, such that a high score still underrepresents an examinee's true level of a trait?

Content validity in this case concerns the relationship between the concept of spiritual abuse and the content of the 66 items that were piloted to form the SHAS. What is spiritual abuse? What are its important parts? What counts as evidence of it? Is there more to spiritual abuse than was captured by the 66 items?

\hypertarget{content-specification}{%
\section{Content Specification}\label{content-specification}}

``Test developers often work from a specification of the content domain. The content specification carefully describes the content in detail, often with a classification of areas of content and types of items'' \citep{Standards}. For the purpose of piloting items, the domain of spiritual abuse included two constructs: external (abusive) events and internal states (of emotional harm).

\hypertarget{external-events}{%
\subsection{External Events}\label{external-events}}

One domain of spiritual abuse is external abuse \emph{events}. The focus is on something that happened, or a pattern of recurring behavior on the part of someone in a place of spiritual authority that was experienced by the examinee as abusive. The first 52 items of the SHAS were drawn from this domain of possible abusive events. To each item, an examinee was offered five response categories:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Never
\item
  Once or twice
\item
  Sometimes
\item
  Often
\item
  Always
\end{enumerate}

To sum each examinee's vector of responses to these items is to accumulate evidence of abuse. In the aggregate, scores for this collection of items could range from 52 to 260. An examinee who \emph{never} experienced \emph{any} of these events would score 52. At the other extreme, an examinee who scored 260 had \emph{always} experienced \emph{all} of these events.

\hypertarget{internal-states}{%
\subsection{Internal States}\label{internal-states}}

Another important domain of spiritual abuse is \emph{internal states}. The focus is on the subjective experience of an examinee ``as a result of negative experiences in church or Christian group settings.'' The questionnaire included 14 items asking examinees about their emotions in response to events. To each item, an examinee was offered the same five response categories as before:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Never
\item
  Once or twice
\item
  Sometimes
\item
  Often
\item
  Always
\end{enumerate}

To sum each examinee's vector of responses to these items is to accumulate evidence of internal emotional \emph{harm}. Total scores for these items could range from 14 to 70. Examinees who \emph{never} experienced \emph{any} of these unpleasant emotions would score 14, while examinees who scored 70 had \emph{always} experienced \emph{all} of the difficult emotions.

\hypertarget{data-collection}{%
\chapter{Data Collection}\label{data-collection}}

The data were collected online from January 28, 2021 to February 27, 2021.

A final total of 3,222 people responded to the survey at some level. Depending on how much data are missing, this is an impressively large sample size which will provide lots of power for most simple statistics and should for the most part satisfy the large-sample requirements of the advanced statistical procedures associated with measurement work (i.e., factor analysis, IRT).

However, the data come from a convenience sample which raises some challenge of sampling bias. Dan sent the link out to {[}lots of people{]}, and (if memory serves) encouraged them to send it to people \emph{they} knew. This would seem to bias responses toward people demographically similar to Dan: white, Millennial, west coast American, middle class, highly educated, primarily Protestant and Catholic Christian, etc. This will be more apparent upon closer inspection of the demographics of the sample.

One source of examinees was people who listen to progressive Christians podcasts such as \emph{You Have Permission} which Dan produced at the time of data collection. This is a known source of bias toward people who have some interest in Christianity along with at least some level of rejection of conservative/orthodox Christianity and/or political conservatism. However, this would be an appropriate group from which to invite participation if the target population is people who have experienced spiritual abuse.

\hypertarget{item-analyses-in-item-response-theory}{%
\section{Item analyses in item response theory}\label{item-analyses-in-item-response-theory}}

Item response theory (IRT) is an established framework for empirically evaluating what items measure \citep{EmbretsonReise2000, WrightMasters1982}. IRT methods complement methods derived from classical test theory in several ways.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Examinees and items on the same equal-interval scale}. In classical test theory, examinees differ in their scores and items differ in their means or percent of examinees selecting a response; examinees and items are on different scales. By contrast, IRT methods allow measurement of persons and items on the \textbf{\emph{same scale}} with \emph{equal interval properties} of the \emph{logit} scale** and resulting linear measures (Wright \& Stone, 1999). In IRT we can estimate \emph{item parameters independently of the characteristics of the calibrating sample}, and we can estimate \emph{person parameters apart from the difficulties of the items taken} (Masters, 1982; Rasch, 1966).
\item
  \textbf{Item separation has diagnostic value}. A core assumption of CTT is that items are \emph{redundant}; they contribute meaningful variation to a total score but they do not occupy a distinct place on the score continuum. IRT is different. IRT does not assume that items are redundant; instead it investigates whether items make distinct contributions to the measurement of the latent trait.
\end{enumerate}

Whereas classical test theory measurement is principally concerned with the ratio of noise to signal in the variation contributed by an item, IRT models compute the probability of a certain response to an item given the level of the latent construct the individual possesses (i.e., spiritual abuse) and the relevant item's difficulty of endorsement. IRT is based on the logistic model and operates on the \emph{logit} scale.

In what follows, we conduct item analyses through a comparison of several different IRT models: the Partial Credit Model (PCM), the Generalized Partial Credit Model (GPCM), and the Rating Scale Model (RSM). I credit Okan Bulut \citeyearpar{Bulut2020} for his excellent tutorial illustrating how to carry out IRT analyses in R using the \texttt{mirt} package (cite mirt).

\hypertarget{irt-model-assumptions}{%
\subsection{IRT Model Assumptions}\label{irt-model-assumptions}}

IRT makes two assumptions of the data. The first is unidimensionality of the latent trait. Is the trait one dimension? This is important for the SHAS because the questionnaire explicitly distinguished between items measuring external events and internal states. To check this assumption we first confirm the factor structure using confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) and Rasch Principal Component Analysis of Residuals (PCAR; Smith, 2002).

The second assumption is local independence \citep{EmbretsonResise2000}. This means that any set of items should not share any meaningful correlation once the latent variable has been accounted for (Edwards, Houts, \& Cai, 2018).
To check the assumption of local independence, we used the Q3 statistic (Yen, 1984). The Q3 statistic index criteria are that the raw residual correlation between pairs of items should never exceed 0.10 (Marais \& Andrich, 2008).

Using these criteria, we found XX items having local dependence. The items that had the highest raw residual correlations had negative signs and no positive residual correlation. In other words, the assumption of local independence in this study was met.

\hypertarget{partial-credit-model-pcm}{%
\subsection{Partial Credit Model (PCM)}\label{partial-credit-model-pcm}}

The first model we examine is the Partial Credit Model (PCM) \citep{Masters1982}. This is a polytomous item response model belonging to the Rasch family. This means that it extends the Rasch model, which is based on dichotomous item responses (1=correct response, 0=incorrect response), by considering the probabliity of choosing from among several response options such as those typically presented in Likert scales of agreement or frequency. The PCM assumes that items use ordered response categories as they exist in questionnaires, such as unidimensional rating scales. The number of response categories in each item may vary \citep{EmbretsonReise2000}. It follows that the PCM contains m (m + 1 being the number of response categories) threshold parameters. Each threshold parameter marks a category intersection \citep{Masters1982}. Threshold sometimes refers to ``step difficulty'' or ``step parameter'' and illustrates the point on the latent trait continuum (e.g., spiritual abuse) where a response in category k becomes more likely than a response in category k -- 1 (de Ayala, 2009). The PCM provides scores for items, persons, and step parameters on a logit scale.

\textbf{Item parameters}. Table \ref{tab:mirt-items} reports the item parameters estimated by the PCM. The first column shows the discrimination parameter, which is equal to ``1'' for all items because the Partial Credit Model, similar to the Rasch model, constrains the discrimination parameter to be ``1''. In the following columns (b1 to b4) are the estimated thresholds or step parameters. Because the SHAS items all have five response categories, the Partial Credit Model estimates four threshold parameters (b1 to b4) for each item. The question here is the spread of the parameters across the range of the trait. In the PCM, ``the property of ordered threshold parameters is not a requirement in the partial credit or generalized partial credit models'' \citep{EmbretsonReise2000}.

Consider the first item: Q7. The thresholds are as follows: b1=0.23, b2=0.08, b3=0.74, b4=1.11. The threshold for b1 is higher than b2. This means examinees who experienced more spiritual abuse overall were more likely to choose ``Never'' than ``Once or twice'' on this item.

\textbf{Item fit}. Two fit statistics assess the fit of an item to the PCM: the infit mean square (MNSQ) and the outfit mean square (MNSQ). The infit statistic places greater emphasis on unexpected responses that are close to the examinees and item location. The outfit is sensitive to unexpected responses that are far from the location \citep{BondFox2015}. The infit and outfit values identify potential unexpected response patterns. The expected value of infit or outfit for each item is 1.0, with a range of acceptable values ranging from 0.5 to 1.5. Values outside this boundary indicate a lack of fit between items and models.

\textbf{Item plots}. We can use also examine these item statistics visually. The following curves are called category response curves because they represent ``the probability of an examinee (or survey respondent) responding in a particular category conditional on trait level'' \citep{EmbretsonReise2000}. In general, ``the higher the slope parameters, the steeper the operating characteristic curves and the more narrow and peaked the category response curves, indicating that the response categories differentiate among trait levels fairly well'' \citep{EmbretsonReise2000}. Polytomous IRT models such as the Partial Credit Model have option characteristic curves (OCCs) which can be considered as an extension of item characteristic curves (ICCs) for polytomous items. Because the items have more than two response categories, OCCs have multiple curves in the same plot. Each curve represents the probability of selecting a particular response option as a function of the latent trait.

Table X ``contains estimation results and the fit index model from each item of the SHAS.'' The estimates of item difficulty were between -X.XX TO X.XX (ie., -1.36 to 1.51) on the logit scale. Based on the item discrimination index (PTBIS; point-biserial correlation), which is analogous to classical test theory (item to-total-correlation), X SHAS items have high discrimination values in a positive direction, with no values below 0.30 or negative values. This indicates that XX items function well to distinguish persons with high versus low levels of spiritual abuse. For each item, we examined the step parameter of category endorsements, and the statistics of each response for the items. X items, except item X, followed the step ordering requirement. We found Item X (Step 1 = -0.XX, Step 2 = -0.XX, Step 3 = 0.XX) experienced threshold disordering as the thresholds were not ordered from lowest to highest. However, none of the infit and outfit statistics for response categories were greater than 2. This finding indicated that collapsing categories was not necessary because the disordered threshold still fit and did not violate the Rasch model (Linacre, 2010; 2018). Based on this information, we concluded that, in general, the SHAS items (fit)/(did not fit) the Rasch PCM model.

In this chapter, we addresses the reliability of the SHAS based on the 66 piloted items. We begin by defining reliability and discussing its importance. Then we report the results of our analyses of the reliability of the piloted SHAS items from both classical test theory (CTT) and item response theory (IRT) frameworks. Since these are cross-sectional data taken at one point in time, our work is limited to \emph{internal consistency} reliability which focuses on the relationships between the items at one point in time rather than relationships between items or scores between two or more time points. We conclude by considering what this information contributes to improved SHAS instrumentation.

\hypertarget{about-reliability}{%
\section{About reliability}\label{about-reliability}}

Reliability is the extent to which items and their scores are consistently measuring the trait of interest. It is evidence that two examinees who provide the same responses to the same items, and by extension receive the same total scores, truly have the same level or amount of, or exposure to, the trait.

\hypertarget{reliability-in-classical-test-theory-ctt}{%
\section{Reliability in Classical Test Theory (CTT)}\label{reliability-in-classical-test-theory-ctt}}

One way to think about reliability in classical test theory is the ratio of signal to noise in the observed data. Do the various item responses and scores provide a true sort of examinees on the trait, or is all this variation merely noise?

The foundation of internal consistency reliability in CTT is relationships between item responses: correlations. Strong correlations among the items are evidence that the items are measuring the same construct. Thus we begin by examining correlation matrices.

\hypertarget{reliability-in-item-response-theory-irt}{%
\section{Reliability in Item Response Theory (IRT)}\label{reliability-in-item-response-theory-irt}}

Wright and Masters (1982) developed reliability measures based on the Rasch measurement model, which have a different concept from classical test theory (e.g., Cronbach's alpha). Reliability is estimated both for persons and items, by means of Person Separation Reliability (PSR). This is an estimate of how well this instrument can distinguish respondents on the measured variable. In parallel, Item Separation Reliability (ISR) is an indication of how well items are separated by the persons taking the test (Wright \& Stone, 1999). The cutoff of separation reliabilities is \textgreater{} 0.80 (Bond \& Fox, 2015).

\hypertarget{about-dimensionality}{%
\section{About Dimensionality}\label{about-dimensionality}}

Dimensionality is the study of the dimensions (also known as ``factors'', ``components'', and ``latent traits'') that the items are designed and assembled to collectively measure. This study tends to take two forms. One is exploration of what dimensions a set of items apparently measure, regardless of what they intended to measure. The other is what evidence exist to confirm that a set of items actually measure the dimension they are intended to measure.

Dimensionality is important for the reporting of scores. Scores, as summary measures of a set of items, are only meaningful to the extent that they enjoy evidence that they measure the dimension(s) they are designed to measure. Scores, in turn, are important to the extent that they support decisions that have consequences in the real world. Scores are needed to decide who gets to practice medicine, or fly an airplane.

Dimensionality is an important part of the validation process for psychological measures \citep{EmbretsonReise2000}.

The methodology commonly used to investigate dimensionality is factor analysis. To assess the dimensionality of these 66 piloted SHAS items we use exploratory factor analysis and confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) and PCAR.

\hypertarget{confirmatory-factor-analysis-cfa}{%
\section{Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)}\label{confirmatory-factor-analysis-cfa}}

The purpose of confirmatory factor analysis is, as the name implies, to confirm that items measure the factor(s) or trait(s) for which the items were designed and intended to measure.

In our use of CFA we used several statistics and fit indices:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Root Mean Square Error of Approximation (RMSEA)
\item
  Comparative Fit Index (CFI)
\item
  Tucker--Lewis Index (TLI), and
\item
  Standardized Root-Mean-Square Residual (SRMR)
\end{itemize}

Based on published criteria (Hu \& Bentler, 1999; Wang \& Wang, 2019), we used the following standards for good fit:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  CFI \textgreater{} 0.95
\item
  TLI \textgreater{} 0.95
\item
  RMSEA \textless{} 0.05
\item
  SRMR \textless{} 0.08
\end{itemize}

We use the MLM estimator \citep{Brown2006}.

The item-person map (also known as the Wright map) is a tool for visualizing the results of a calibration of items and persons. The Wright map was originally developed for use in test development but it also helps illuminate the results of surveys \citep{bulut20215}. As \citet{bulut20215} explains:

\emph{``The Partial Credit Model (PCM), which is an extension of the Rasch model for polytomous item responses, can be used for survey development and validation (Green \& Frantom, 2002). We can analyze survey items using PCM and obtain item thresholds for each item. These thresholds indicate the magnitude of the latent trait required to select a particular response option (e.g., selecting disagree over strongly disagree). Using an item-person map, we can see thresholds for the items as well as the distribution of the latent trait for the respondents. This plot is quite useful for analyzing the alignment (or match) between the items and respondents answering the items.''}
