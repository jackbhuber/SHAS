\hypertarget{abstract}{%
\section{Abstract}\label{abstract}}

This is a reproducible research project for validation of the nascent Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS), developed by Dan Koch. It aims to function as an evolving technical report to conceptualize and document various validity studies -- and, by extension, validity evidence -- for this scale. We hope some of these validity considerations and empirical studies amount to meaningful publishable contributions to the nascent research literature in spiritual abuse and/or the literature in religion, counseling psychology, and/or psychological measurement.

The project is open source, for collaboration. It is built in R using the R Studio IDE \citep{R-base}. No Excel, no SPSS, no Word. It is built in \texttt{bookdown} (\citet{R-bookdown}) and hosted at Github as a public repository at \url{https://github.com/jackbhuber/SHAS}. There it is served over Github Pages as a website -- \url{https://jackhuber.org/SHAS/} -- for collaboration between Jack and Dan.

\textbf{Research Questions}

Validation is research into the meanings and uses of scores from instruments. It can be an ongoing research agenda. Following is an initial set of questions to guide research and scale improvement of the SHAS:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Item analysis and refinement} - Do some items have too many response categories?
\item
  \textbf{Data reduction} - As written and executed, which items appear to be critical (best performing) and which appear to be redundant or noisy? What might a shorter, streamlined instrument look like?
\item
  \textbf{Person fit} - How well does the current scale fit the population of examinees? Are there ceiling or floor effects? To what extent might the existing items under-represent the full range of abuse?
\item
  \textbf{Scale invariance} - Do the scales mean the same thing to different groups of examinees? To what extent do these scales (abuse constructs) vary by age, gender, and/or sexuality?
\item
  \textbf{Developmental pathway} - Beyond the current conceptualization of scales composed of redundant items, what might an incremental scale of abuse with diagnostic items look like?
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{introduction}{%
\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}}

Spiritual and religious abuse is a new construct to describe a serious problem. As \citet{KochEdstrom2022} put it:

\begin{quote}
The terms ``spiritual abuse'' and ``religious abuse'' are used interchangeably by researchers. Oakley et al.~(2018) define spiritual abuse as a form of emotional and psychological abuse characterized by systemic patterns of coercion and control in religious contexts; spiritual abuse is distinguished from broad emotional abuse by its common inclusion of specifically religious elements, like the quoting of scripture or the giving of a ``divine rationale'' for mistreatment. (Much like R/S abuse, there is no consensus definition of emotional abuse, but Pearl (1994) proposed the following categories: rejecting, isolating, terrorizing, ignoring, corrupting, verbally assaulting, and overpressuring.) Swindle (2017) casts a wider net and identifies three broad categories of religious abuse: abuse perpetrated by religious leaders, abuse perpetrated by a religious group or person representing a religious group, and abuse with a religious or spiritual component. Other definitions focus on specific forms of religious or spiritual abuse, like using scripture or religious language to justify domestic abuse (Cares \& Cusick, 2012; Warford, 2014), any sexual or physically abusive act done by a clergy member (Doyle, 2006), and theologically sanctioned mistreatment of LGBTQ persons (Foster, Bowland, \& Vosler, 2015). Taken together, R/S abuse is distinguished from other types of abuse by a religious component of one sort or another. As conceived for this article, combining the approaches of Oakley, et al.~(2018) and Swindle (2017), spiritual abuse is defined as a type of emotional and psychological abuse perpetrated by a religious leader or group and/or with a religious or spiritual component, usually involving coercion or control. It is separable from other types of abuse that may co-ccur or take place in a religious context or by a religious figure, like clergy sexual abuse or domestic violence excused through the quoting of scripture. A survivor of clergy sexual abuse, then, is understood for our purposes to have been abused both sexually and spiritually. The sexual abuse can be expected to have effects on the survivor similar to other instances of sexual abuse, while the spiritual abuse will likely affect whether or how they are able to practice their faith.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Based on the existing qualitative research, the negative effects of spiritual abuse can be very serious. Ward (2011) found evidence of persistent physical illness in multiple respondents, including major surgery, chronic hospital visits, gallstones, biliary colic of the liver, and inflamed esophagus, understood to be a result of stress-induced exhaustion from spending time in spiritually abusive environments. Swindle (2017) found that all seven of her participants reported some level of emotional trauma; psychological consequences included depression, anger, fear, feeling stuck, confusion, and decreased self-worth. One participant was shunned and banned from all church group activities when it was discovered that she was being treated with depression medication and seeing a psychiatrist (Swindle 2017). Many of the respondents in a survey by Oakley \& Kinmond (2014) reported leaders who demanded complete, unquestioning obedience; a consensus that a leader is divinely appointed to that position can leave abusers unchallenged. Abuse by religious figures appears to be quite destructive. Stevens et al.~(2019) found that college students who alleged sexual abuse by religious figures were just as symptomatic as those abused by their parents. It can be inferred from this finding that religious leaders have a strong, near-parent-level potential for abusive harm, but more research is needed to support this inference.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
There are also concerns related to secondary trauma for clinicians working with R/S abused clients. In a small study of five counselors who had worked with spiritually abused clients, Gubi \& Jacobs (2009) found symptoms of secondary traumatic stress disorder and visceral traumatization, including anger, sadness, outrage, grief, cynicism, and helplessness. Encouragingly, working with these clients also profoundly deepened the counselor's relationship with God (Gubi \& Jacobs 2009). Increasing visibility and training around R/S abuse may lead to protections and benefits not only for survivors but practitioners as well.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
However defined, trauma occurring in a R/S setting has the effect of reducing a victim's ability to use R/S as a coping strategy, at least for those who exercise their R/S in organized contexts (Panchuk, 2018). This may be the result of religious leaders explicitly claiming to represent God (Ward, 2011) or simply of the abuse occurring in a religious context and therefore carrying traumatic connotations for the survivor. Given the broad evidential support for the role of R/S in healing from trauma, it is especially pernicious that R/S abuse takes place in religious settings of such strong potential for growth and healing.
\end{quote}

In light of these concerns, a valid scale of spiritual harm and abuse is needed. Such a scale may provide myriad potential benefits. Practitioners of clients whom they suspect may have been the victims of R/S abuse could administer the scale as part of their intake process, enabling them to quickly check their client's score against established, valid, and reliable norms. A reliable measurement of exposure to potential spiritual abuse and its effects can also be used extensively for research purposes, looking for relationships between spiritual abuse and any number of other psychological, health, or other measures. Their research question was: ``Which types of R/S abuse most succinctly capture the phenomenon of being broadly exposed to R/S abuse?'' The hypothesis is that factor analysis will reveal \textless30 items that can reliably comprise a shorter scale for broad exposure to religious and spiritual abuse and its common effects.

To date only one scale has been developed to measure religious or spiritual abuse \citet{Keller2016} and this scale has not been used in any subsequent peer-reviewed research. Other scales measure various types of abuse, but not R/S abuse specifically. The Cumulative Trauma Scale (Kira, et al.~2008) was developed to measure overall trauma amongst a Middle Eastern refugee population. The Child Abuse and Trauma Scale (Sanders \& Becker-Lausen, 1995) measures childhood and adolescent experiences. The Trauma History Screen (Carlson, et al.~2011) includes a handful of items that could be religion-related, like sexual abuse or being ``suddenly abandoned by a spouse, partner, parent, or family'' but does not ask about the cause of the abandonment. The Trauma Symptom Checklist (Briere \& Runtz, 1989) measures a respondent's current trauma levels and contains helpful subsets of trauma, but no items ask specifically about anything religious. Other trauma-related screens and scales similarly lack any specific religious component.

\citet{KochEdstrom2022} laid the foundation for a scale measuring exposure to potentially spiritually abusive experiences. They defined the construct of spiritual harm and abuse and developed an initial item pool. They conducted EFA which revealed a handful of factors which they used to reduce the number of items and to define several subscales.

This paper extends on \citet{KochEdstrom} by using additional psychometric evidence to gather additional evidence of validity for the nascent Spiritual Harm \& Abuse Scale (SHAS). The goal of the current study was to establish an outcome measure that reflects a patient's exposure to and damage from spiritual harm and abuse. We aimed to establish an item bank on healthcare engagement that is sufficiently unidimensional to yield a single score, is free from differential item functioning based on race, gender, or chronic condition, and maximizes parsimony and precision across the continuum of the latent construct.

\section{Methods}\label{methods}}

\hypertarget{item-pool-development}{%
\subsection{Item Pool Development}\label{item-pool-development}}

We developed a Spiritual Harm and Abuse item pool. We previously validated the spiritual harm and abuse construct using a literature review, expert reviews, and qualitative concept elicitation interviews with people.

The item pool for this study (66 items) was generated by mapping items from publicly available measures of similar constructs and writing new items to achieve construct coverage.

\begin{quote}
All items were written or edited to a 6th grade reading level. Cognitive interviews with 56 patients included think aloud responses, prompts for comprehension, retrieval, judgement or other difficulties responding to items, and relevance {[}22{]}. The a priori standard for item inclusion was three consecutive interviews establishing comprehension, relevance, and no difficulties. Minor wording changes were based on consensus of study interviewers and the principal investigator, major wording changes were addressed in dedicated item review meetings of the study staff, investigators, and consulting primary care or mental health providers.
\end{quote}

\hypertarget{participants-and-procedures}{%
\subsection{Participants and Procedures}\label{participants-and-procedures}}

\begin{quote}
We conducted an online survey of listeners to progressive Christian podcasts. Our sampling priorities for this initial quantitative validation were (a) variability across the full range of the construct; and (b) representation of diverse clinical samples from target populations for the measure's use {[}23{]}. The sampling frame was identified from administrative data in the VA Corporate Data Warehouse {[}24{]}. We sampled from the VA primary care population, who are predominantly male, and older, with poorer health status, and less racially diverse as compared to other healthcare populations {[}25{]}. Inclusion criteria were: veteran, aged 18--80, White or Black race (Asian, Pacific Islander, and Native Americans are each≤1\% of users and accuracy in public sector administrative data is poor {[}26{]}), a valid mailing address in the medical record, at least one past-year primary care visit to the assigned primary care panel, and a diagnosis of one of four prevalent chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension, depression, PTSD), as indicated by one inpatient stay or two outpatient visits on different dates of service. Exclusion criteria were active diagnoses of dementia or psychotic disorders, or 90-day risk for hospitalization or death in the top 5\% as indicated by the VA's Care Assessment Needs score {[}27{]}. Stratified sampling was used to ensure sufficient cell size for subgroup comparisons and DIF analyses. Sampling was stratified by chronic condition cohort, and within cohorts to oversample Women, Black veterans and Hispanic veterans. Cohorts were also balanced on health status, as indicated by risk adjustment scores {[}28{]}. Participants were consented before beginning the survey. The IRB at Northwest University. The data were collected from January 28, 2021 to February 27, 2021.
\end{quote}

\hypertarget{measures}{%
\subsection{Measures}\label{measures}}

\textbf{Spiritual Harm and Abuse items}

Koch tested 66 candidate items representing the two domains of the spiritual harm and abuse construct: external events (52 items) and internal states (14 items). The measure asks respondents to frequency with which they experienced each event or state. Candidate items were provisionally scored on a 5-point Likert-type scale, with response options of ``Never,'' ``Once or twice,'' ``Sometimes,'' ``Often,'' and ``All the time.''

\hypertarget{data-analysis}{%
\subsection{Data Analysis}\label{data-analysis}}

\begin{quote}
Our analysis strategy was to pare down the item pool to reflect the latent construct of healthcare engagement. Although our theoretical model was composed of four inter-related domains of engagement behaviors, engagement was hypothesized to stem from generalized self-efficacy across these behaviors {[}16{]}, making the common variance across domains our primary interest, rather than comprehensive coverage of each domain that would result in separate scales or subscales. We assumed an effect-indicator model from the beginning of the analysis {[}32{]}, and therefore, the analysis strategy also targeted removal of smaller, extraneous factors in an effort to identify a sufficiently unidimensional set of items that could form a single scale. A different set of theoretical assumptions, such as creating an inventory of knowledge and skills, could be consistent with a causal-indicator model, where the focus of item selection would be to maximize the unique content represented by each subdomain {[}33{]}. While there are merits of each approach, we felt the latter would have contributed to proliferation of similar constructs in the field, overemphasized each subdomain's independence, increased respondent burden, and would have had relatively limited utility in healthcare systems due to the need to reliably assess and interpret multiple domains and scores.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Preliminary analyses examined classical item-level statistics, polychoric correlations, and hierarchical cluster analysis {[}34{]} to identify poorly functioning items. Next, we conducted iterative exploratory bifactor analyses using the psych package in R. We started by selecting four subfactors corresponding to the four hypothesized domains of the construct but considered solutions with three subfactors as well. (For a detailed illustration of how to apply bifactor modeling to patient experience data, see Reise et al.~{[}35{]}) We evaluated dimensionality based on general factor saturation and computed Omega-Hierarchical (Omega-H), where values \textgreater{} 0.70 suggest sufficient unidimensionality for IRT modeling {[}36{]}. In addition, we calculated explained common variance (ECV), the relative strength of a general factor compared to group factors, targeting ECV values \textgreater{} 0.60 as a tentative benchmark {[}36{]}.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
As a final step, we used a statistical learning theory (SLT) approach to optimize the parsimony of the item pool, minimize expected prediction error, and incorporate criterion validity into the item selection process {[}37{]}. SLT methods have good potential to inform the development of latent trait scales. The measurement of complex constructs, such as patient engagement, requires investigators to balance the theoretical and conceptual basis of the construct with empirical results in making judgments in the presence of structural ambiguity {[}38{]}. Well-documented pitfalls are associated with too great an emphasis on optimizing inter-item correlations, such as the proliferation of reliable but narrow scales or subscales that under-represent a construct {[}39{]}.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
In our context, the SLT procedure helped us to identify items that could be deleted because they were not sufficiently related to criterion variables. We derived a cross-validated set of items that correlated with the validity indicators for each of the three domains of the model using the Best Items Scale that is Cross Validated, Unit-weighted, Informative, and Transparent (BISCUIT), which has been shown to produce parsimonious models with comparable levels of predictive accuracy, even at high levels of missing data when compared to other statistical learning techniques {[}40{]}. The tuning parameter was selected to exert a moderate degree of influence on item selection, identifying the best 20 items for each criterion, with 1000 bootstrap replications. All items that appeared in optimal scales for all three validity indicators in \textgreater{} 1 replication were retained in the item pool.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
To illustrate the structure of our final set of items prior to IRT analyses, we ran a confirmatory bifactor model corresponding to the final structure determined by exploratory factor analyses. For this final model, we report Omega-H, ECV, Comparative Fit Index (CFI), the Tucker Lewis Index (TLI), and the root-mean-square error of approximation (RMSEA). Next, we estimated IRT parameters using the graded response model (GRM) {[}41{]}, as well as the corresponding IRT-based bifactor model with three subfactors {[}42{]}. We did this with the understanding that unidimensional parameters might still be distorted by subfactors evident in a bifactor model. Therefore, we applied a procedure to convert conditional general factor slopes into marginal slopes, using formulas 10 and 11 provided in a didactic article by Toland et al.~{[}43{]}, citing detailed work on these procedures {[}44{]}. Once in marginal form, the slopes and location parameters from the bifactor model can be fairly compared to unidimensional models, and may be scored ``as if'' they were from unidimensional models.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Differential item functioning (DIF) analysis compared race (Black/African-American vs.~White), age (65 and above vs below 65), mental health vs physical health cohorts, and financial strain. We applied logistic ordinal regression with IRT scoring, commonly used in the development of PROMIS instruments {[}45{]}. We used the Chi-squared likelihood-ratio statistic as the initial DIF detection criteria (alpha \textless{} 0.01), and a cut-off of McFadden pseudo R2Δ ≥ 0.02 in model comparisons to determine substantial DIF, a reasonable threshold used in the development of self-reported health outcomes {[}46{]}.
\end{quote}

All analyses were conducted in \textbf{R}, using the \textbf{psych} package {[}47{]} and \textbf{lavaan} {[}48{]} for factor analyses, \textbf{mirt} {[}49{]}for IRT analyses and \textbf{lordif} {[}45{]} for DIF analyses.

\hypertarget{initial-item-analysis}{%
\subsubsection{Initial Item Analysis}\label{initial-item-analysis}}

\begin{quote}
XXX pairs of items in the polychoric correlation matrix were highly correlated (r \textgreater{} 0.70). After removing X items, these pairs disappeared (several items appeared in multiple pairs). We note that these pairs of items were expected because we tested several items with alternate wording. Next, we examined how individual items (or small groups of items) might separate from larger sets of items with a hierarchical item cluster plot {[}34{]}. This plot showed final clustering of a pair of items with narrow content (social support) and a single item that had poor wording and the lowest cluster loading (i.e., corrected item-total correlation). We deleted these three items, and an additional item with redundant wording, leaving XX in the item pool at this stage.
\end{quote}

\hypertarget{exploratory-bifactor-analysis}{%
\subsection{Exploratory Bifactor Analysis}\label{exploratory-bifactor-analysis}}

\begin{quote}
We removed a total of nine items in this step, based on empirical and conceptual considerations. The bifactor models with four subfactors identified three items with low general factor loadings (\textless{} 0.50) or high specific factor loadings (\textgreater{} 0.50). These items focused on individual health behavior maintenance. In addition, we noticed that a single specific factor was driven by three items from the ``health information use'' domain, also showing high specific factor loadings and low general factor loadings, suggesting a potential for multidimensionality. They focused on independent health information use (e.g., ``It is easy to find information on my own that helps me manage my symptoms''). All other items in this domain showed lower loadings on the ``health information use'' factor (\textless{} 0.35). We opted to remove these three items, and model three subfactors representing the remaining subdomains. This decision to shift from 4 to 3 subfactors was based not only on these empirical considerations, but on the overarching conceptual assumption that the construct of engagement is essentially collaborative, and that the multidimensionality of items addressing independent skills was inconsistent with this assumption.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
In the subsequent 3-factor bifactor model, we identified three more items with general factor loadings of 0.50 or less. These items addressed resilient self-efficacy for independent self-management behaviors (e.g., ``I attend all of my appointments, even when life gets busy or stressful'') and were removed based on similar empirical considerations and conceptual assumptions. The resulting 30 item set showed a high proportion of general factor variance relative to specific factor variance (item-level proportions ranging from 0.58 to 0.83), with overall indices of Omega-H as 0.79 and ECV 0.69. These values suggest sufficient levels of unidimensionality and that the items mostly reflect a single construct {[}36{]}.
\end{quote}
