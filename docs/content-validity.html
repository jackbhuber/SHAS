Validity Evidence for the Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS)

Content Validity

In this chapter, we consider the content validity of the Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS). We begin by defining content validity and discussing its importance. Then we document the work we did to map the domain of spiritual abuse for the purpose of developing items for piloting and operational use. We conclude by considering what scope exists for more work in this area that would improve future development of SHAS instrumentation.

About Content Validity

The focus of content validity is “the relationship between the content of a test and the construct it is intended to measure” (American Educational Research Association, American Psychological Association, and National Council on Measurement in Education 2014). Do they match? Does the instrument contain content that is not part of the construct? Are there parts of the content unmeasured, such that a high score still underrepresents an examinee’s true level of a trait?

Content validity in this case concerns the relationship between the concept of spiritual abuse and the content of the 66 items that were piloted to form the SHAS. What is spiritual abuse? What are its important parts? What counts as evidence of it? Is there more to spiritual abuse than was captured by the 66 items?

Content Specification

“Test developers often work from a specification of the content domain. The content specification carefully describes the content in detail, often with a classification of areas of content and types of items” (American Educational Research Association, American Psychological Association, and National Council on Measurement in Education 2014). For the purpose of piloting items, the domain of spiritual abuse included two constructs: external (abusive) events and internal states (of emotional harm).

External Events

One domain of spiritual abuse is external abuse events. The focus is on something that happened, or a pattern of recurring behavior on the part of someone in a place of spiritual authority that was experienced by the examinee as abusive. The first 52 items of the SHAS were drawn from this domain of possible abusive events. To each item, an examinee was offered five response categories:

  1. Never
  2. Once or twice
  3. Sometimes
  4. Often
  5. Always

To sum each examinee’s vector of responses to these items is to accumulate evidence of abuse. In the aggregate, scores for this collection of items could range from 52 to 260. An examinee who never experienced any of these events would score 52. At the other extreme, an examinee who scored 260 had always experienced all of these events.

Internal States

Another important domain of spiritual abuse is internal states. The focus is on the subjective experience of an examinee “as a result of negative experiences in church or Christian group settings.” The questionnaire included 14 items asking examinees about their emotions in response to events. To each item, an examinee was offered the same five response categories as before:

  1. Never
  2. Once or twice
  3. Sometimes
  4. Often
  5. Always

To sum each examinee’s vector of responses to these items is to accumulate evidence of internal emotional harm. Total scores for these items could range from 14 to 70. Examinees who never experienced any of these unpleasant emotions would score 14, while examinees who scored 70 had always experienced all of the difficult emotions.
