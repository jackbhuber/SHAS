Validity Evidence for the Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS)

Validity Evidence for the Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS)

Jack Huber, Swedish Medical Center

Dan Koch, Northwest University

May 2022

Abstract

Spiritual harm and abuse has been a serious problem that has begun to gain the attention of the clinical and psychological research community. Some research has documented lasting emotional and psychological damage to victims of spiritual abuse. A valid scale of spiritual and religious abuse is needed, and the nascent Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS) has potential to meet this need. Koch and Edstrom (2022) developed an item pool and conducted an initial validation study of the SHAS which established the multidimensional structure of the item pool. This study builds on Koch and Edstrom (2022) to further validate the SHAS by using item response theory (IRT). The goal of this study was to enhance the SHAS with several of these features of IRT. Provided that the items were sufficiently unidimensional, we aimed to express the SHAS as a single interval-level outcome measure of a patient’s lifetime exposure to, and damage from, spiritual harm and abuse. Because in IRT the scale describes items as well as persons, we aimed to further develop the potential diagnostic value of the SHAS items by illustrating how they individually assign real-world meaning to locations on the scale as well how they cluster with other items to measure some parts of the scale with more precision than others. Such a scale also lays the foundation to further develop the SHAS by identifying anchor items that can calibrate with pilot items on future forms. Finally, we aimed to establish a SHAS score that is free of differential item functioning based on race, gender, and other demographic variables, and maximizes parsimony and precision across the continuum of the latent construct.
