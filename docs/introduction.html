Validity Evidence for the Spiritual Harm and Abuse Scale (SHAS)

Introduction

Spiritual and religious abuse is a new construct to describe a serious problem.

As Koch and Edstrom (2022) put it:

Research to date on spiritual and religious abuse is a wild west. Taken together, R/S abuse is distinguished from other types of abuse by a religious component of one sort or another. A form of emotional and psychological abuse characterized by systemic patterns of coercion and control in religious contexts; spiritual abuse is distinguished from broad emotional abuse by its common inclusion of specifically religious elements, like the quoting of scripture or the giving of a “divine rationale” for mistreatment (Oakley et al, 2018). Categories: rejecting, isolating, terrorizing, ignoring, corrupting, verbally assaulting, and over-pressuring (Pearl, 1994). Three broad categories of religious abuse: abuse perpetrated by religious leaders, abuse perpetrated by a religious group or person representing a religious group, and abuse with a religious or spiritual component (Swindle, 2017). Other definitions focus on specific forms of religious or spiritual abuse, like using scripture or religious language to justify domestic abuse (Cares & Cusick, 2012; Warford, 2014), any sexual or physically abusive act done by a clergy member (Doyle, 2006), and theologically sanctioned mistreatment of LGBTQ persons (Foster, Bowland, & Vosler, 2015).

Koch and Edstrom (2022) define spiritual abuse as “a type of emotional and psychological abuse perpetrated by a religious leader or group and/or with a religious or spiritual component, usually involving coercion or control.” It is separable from other types of abuse that may co-occur or take place in a religious context or by a religious figure, like clergy sexual abuse or domestic violence excused through the quoting of scripture. A survivor of clergy sexual abuse, then, is understood for our purposes to have been abused both sexually and spiritually. The sexual abuse can be expected to have effects on the survivor similar to other instances of sexual abuse, while the spiritual abuse will likely affect whether or how they are able to practice their faith.

Research points to serious negative effects of spiritual abuse (Ward, 2011; Swindle, 2017; Oakley & Kinmond, 2014; Stevens et al. 2019). This research suggests that eligious leaders have a strong, near-parent-level potential for abusive harm, but more research is needed to support this inference. Further, trauma that takes place in a religious or spiritual setting can rob victims of religion and spirituality as a coping strategy, at least for those who exercise their religion and spirituality in in organized contexts (Panchuk, 2018). Given the broad evidential support for the role of R/S in healing from trauma, it is especially pernicious that R/S abuse takes place in religious settings of such strong potential for growth and healing. There are also concerns related to secondary trauma for clinicians working with R/S abused clients (Gubi & Jacobs, 2009).

In light of these concerns, a valid scale of spiritual harm and abuse is needed. To date one scale has been developed to measure religious or spiritual abuse (Keller2016?) but this instrument has not been used in any subsequent peer-reviewed research. Other scales measure various types of abuse, but not religious and spiritual abuse specifically (Kira, et al. 2008; Sanders & Becker-Lausen, 1995; Carlson, et al. 2011; Briere & Runtz, 1989).

Such a scale may provide myriad potential benefits. Practitioners of clients whom they suspect may have been the victims of R/S abuse could administer the scale as part of their intake process, enabling them to quickly check their client’s score against established, valid, and reliable norms. A reliable measurement of exposure to potential spiritual abuse and its effects can also be used extensively for research purposes, looking for relationships between spiritual abuse and any number of other psychological, health, or other measures.

Koch and Edstrom (2022) laid the foundation for a scale measuring exposure to potentially spiritually abusive experiences. They asked which types of religious and spiritual abuse most succinctly capture the phenomenon of being broadly exposed to religious and spiritual abuse. They defined the construct of spiritual harm and abuse and developed an initial item pool. They conducted EFA which revealed a handful of factors which they used to reduce the number of items and to define several subscales.

This paper builds on Koch and Edstrom (2022) and gathers additional validity evidence for the SHAS by using item response theory.

Although more commonly used with achievement tests, IRT models can also be used for both item calibration and “ability” estimation with rating scale instruments such as questionnaires measuring psychological or behavioral constructs. There are several advantages to using IRT models with rating scale instruments compared to other approaches such as sum scoring. Most importantly, such instruments yield data that are ordinal, not interval, making use of sum scores questionable (Smith, Conrad, Chang, & Piazza, 2002). In Addition, IRT models provide “sample-free measurement estimates, making it possible to estimate a person’s level of the latent construct free of the distribution of the individual items and to estimate an item’s difficulty level free from the distribtion of people used in the sample” (DiStefano and Morgan, 2011, p.356). In addition, as Reeve and Masse (2004) point out, these models allow more in-depth analysis of items, examination of precision across the score continuum (as opposed to a single overall reliability coefficient) and better handling of complex measurement problems such as linking scores across alternative forms” (Dogan 2018, 2).

The goal of the current study was to refine the SHAS and provide an outcome measure that reflects a patient’s exposure to and damage from spiritual harm and abuse. We aimed to establish an item bank on spiritual harm and abuse that is sufficiently uni-dimensional to yield a single score, is free from differential item functioning based on race, gender, and maximizes parsimony and precision across the continuum of the latent construct.
